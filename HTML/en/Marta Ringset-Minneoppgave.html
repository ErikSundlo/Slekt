Marta Ringset – A Memoir

Introduction by Harald Sundlo: The following memoir was written by Marta Baade Ringset for a Sunnmøre project recording the reminiscences of elderly residents, conducted in the 1990s. It is presented here in her own words, translated from the original New Norwegian.

Origins

I was born on the 23rd of April, 1883, at Voksa in Sande. The Baade family had come originally from Germany and Denmark; Daniel Baade had served as senior gauger in Trondheim, his son Peter Daniel had been rural dean of Borgund, and his son Fredrik Kristian had been a first lieutenant who purchased Voksa and acquired an Icelandic wife, one Anne Snorresdottir — which gives the family, one might observe, a more international flavour than is common in Sunnmøre genealogy.

My father, Fredrik Baade, was a farmer and fisherman who was lost in a storm in Breisundet off Ålesund on the 22nd of June, 1896, together with his two eldest sons. I was thirteen years old.

Farm Life at Voksa

Life at Voksa in those years revolved around the sea and the land in equal measure — inshore fishing, coastal fishing, the preparation of stockfish, and all the business of a small farm. We ate salt herring, dried herring, potato dumplings, oat flatbread, and whey cheese; four meals a day. Our clothes and shoes were made at home; the dress for my confirmation was the first thing I ever wore that had been bought from a shop.

Schooling took me to a housewifery school in Ørsta, a two-month weaving course, and a vegetable-dye course — an education that was, in its way, entirely practical and not without its satisfactions.

Youth and Marriage

The youth association, the IOGT lodge at Sandsøy, and the dances in boathouses provided the social fabric of the community. The great Sunnmøre Liberal Youth gatherings — with four or five thousand participants — were occasions of considerable excitement; the teacher Henrik Straumsheim was the idol of that generation.

In 1918 I married Nils E. Ringset; the wedding was held at Ringset. Liabygda at that time had some 250 inhabitants, with small farms running to seven or eight cows, fourteen or fifteen sheep, and a horse. The stone clearance required by the land — 1,200 cartloads per acre — gives some indication of the nature of the work involved.

Nils and Community Life

Nils's community activities were, to use the appropriate understatement, extensive: chairman of Sunnmøre Liberal Youth Federation for 27 years from 1919; thirty years on Stranda parish council; founder of Sunnmøre Museum; mayor 1937–41 and 1945; removed in 1943 for refusing to vote in favour of a salute to Quisling — which one records as a moment of entirely straightforward moral clarity in a period that was not always well supplied with such moments. Chairman of Møre Co-operative Purchasing for 30 years; chairman of the Norwegian Forestry Association for 5 years; exchange visits to Iceland. He received the Civic Medal in silver, the Icelandic Order of the Falcon (1958), and the 'Family Tree' award (1971).

The Lyngvin Housewifery School was managed by a committee that Nils led; they leased the Linge farm in Nordal from 1921 for twenty years. After the war the school had been badly damaged by the Germans; it was repaired and transferred to the county.

Family

We had five children; one son drowned at the age of four. The eldest son took over the farm; a second son became a dairy engineer in Bærum; the eldest daughter became a head nursing sister in Seattle; the second daughter settled in Asker. Leiv Furset, Nils's sister's son, built a furniture factory in Liabygda — his chair 'Pål' sold in excess of half a million units. The factory burned down; a new and larger one was built. I ran a boarding house for the factory workers.

Nils died on the 11th of February, 1976.

Later Years

I remained active until past the age of seventy. The municipal old people's home in Liabygda was inaugurated on the 4th of September, 1980 — all ten of its first residents from the village itself.

Afterword by Harald Sundlo, 12th September, 2008: After Leiv's death, the factory was taken over by his son-in-law; it went into receivership and was then acquired by Jørund Ringset, son of Frøystein, who operated it until 2007, when the business moved to Stordal. The premises in Liabygda are now a garage.
